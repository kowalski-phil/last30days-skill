# Before You Search

This tool will happily take any query and hand you 50 items. Most of them will be useless unless you know what you're looking for.

Here's the lesson, learned the hard way:

> **Define who YOU are before you define what you search.**

Skip this step, get noise. Do it, the same tool produces signal.

---

## The 20-minute strategic session

Before your first query, answer four questions. Not in your head — write them down.

### 1. Who is your audience?

Who are you trying to serve, influence, learn from, or reach? Be specific.

Not "finance people" — *which* finance people. Accountants and AP clerks are a different audience from CFOs and FP&A leads. Different pain, different vocabulary, different forums. Procurement specialists care about vendor management and contract AI. Controllers care about close acceleration. FP&A leads care about forecasting and scenario planning. These are separate conversations.

### 2. What's the job this tool does for you?

Four common ones:

- **Trend radar** — spot emerging themes before the mainstream catches on
- **Content fuel** — story hooks and real-voice quotes for writing
- **Discovery intel** — find people, vendors, and voices worth watching
- **Client prep** — deep briefs before a specific sales call or project

Each job wants a different query style and a different synthesis treatment. Pick one as primary. The secondary jobs fall out naturally.

### 3. What are you trying to become known for?

The tool's queries should reinforce your positioning, not scatter it. If you write about agentic finance, don't run searches on AP invoice matching — that's a different category and a different audience. Every query you run is a micro-bet on your positioning. Make them consistent.

### 4. Narrow, broad, or medium?

- **Too broad** (like `AI training`) returns disconnected piles — biology data infrastructure next to crypto drama next to SQL tutorials next to a solo dev raising an AI on heavy metal.
- **Too narrow** (like `Basware AP automation`) returns existing vendor discourse — great for content fuel, useless for trend radar (if you found it with a narrow query, it's already mainstream).
- **Medium-width** (like `agentic FP&A` or `autonomous finance`) — the domain locks the scope, the concept word locks the emergence angle. Works for most use cases.

Rule of thumb: **field name + emerging concept name**. Not just the field. Not just the concept. Both.

---

## The failure mode

Run this query: `last30days "AI training"`.

You'll get ~50 items across unrelated themes — hardware economics, alignment research, scientific computing, labor/data collection, crypto tangents, accidental keyword matches. Eight unrelated conversations, not one.

That's what happens when you haven't defined your audience or your job.

---

## The working mode

Run this instead: `last30days "agentic FP&A" --search=linkedin,x,youtube,hackernews,reddit`

Same tool, same plumbing. The output will be themed, useful, and directly feedable into your content, positioning, or sales motion — *because the query was rooted in a specific audience and job*.

---

## Rule of thumb

> **The tool is only as good as the prompt, and the prompt is only as good as the person doing the prompting.**

Spend 20 minutes on the strategic session. Save hours on useless reports.
